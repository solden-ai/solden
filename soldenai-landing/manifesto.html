The map is not the territory.

Alfred Korzybski · Science and Sanity, 1933

Solder is the metal alloy that bonds electrical components into circuits. Without solder, components sit next to each other. Current does not flow. The system has every part it needs and still goes dark.

Solder does not replace the components. It joins them. It is invisible when it works and catastrophic when it fails. The most common cause of electronic failure is a broken solder joint: every part still functions, but the signal cannot get across the seam. The bond is the system.

We believe back office operations are at the same point. The systems work. The components are capable. What is missing is the bonding layer between them, the record that holds work together while it crosses the seams between tools, between humans, between time.

Solden is built to hold what humans cannot hold reliably: the work itself. The state, the ownership, the dependencies, the exceptions, the audit trail. Persistent, structured, governed, reversible.

We are building the bonding layer for back office work.

What we believe

  1. Back office work needs a place to live, not another tool to check.
  2. Work in progress belongs to the operator, not the vendor.
  3. Shared memory beats chasing through a chokepoint.
  4. The bond between systems is the system.
  5. Automation executes steps. Durable records keep work alive over time.

This is not a new problem. It is a structural one that has survived four generations of back office technology.

The seams between systems do not hold.

Back office work has moved through four eras. None of them strengthened the joints between components.

Paper.

Solved record-keeping. The bottleneck was physical: you could not act on what you could not reach.

Systems of record.

Solved transactional state. Each system held the record of what happened in its slice, but none knew the work in flight. Approvals lived in email. Exceptions lived in chat. Decisions lived in human memory.

SaaS sprawl.

Solved specialized workflow. A purpose-built tool for every workflow. Each tool optimized one slice while the seams between tools got worse.

AI copilots.

Solved point intelligence. Drafting, extracting, summarizing, recommending. The AI is brilliant for thirty seconds and amnesiac after. It can extract an invoice but it cannot finish the workflow.

Back office work is now entering its fifth era: autonomous agents operating continuously against durable work records.

What automation cannot hold

The default response to back office work has been automation. Trigger an action. Send an approval. Post to a system of record. Notify the team.

Automation is the wrong layer. Automation executes steps. It does not bind work over time.

A back office workflow is not a sequence of tasks. It is a long-lived process with waiting conditions, exceptions, dependencies, reversals, retries, and override windows. The work spans days, weeks, sometimes months.

Systems of record store outcomes. Specialized tools automate slices. Integration platforms trigger actions. Communication tools hold decisions. But no system holds the work itself: the current owner, the next step, the waiting conditions, the retries, the reversals, the operational memory across systems.

When the joint breaks, the system goes silent

Back office workflows have distributed knowledge. The AP clerk knows the vendor. The procurement lead knows the contract terms. The security reviewer knows the access risk. The compliance officer knows the policy boundary. The controller knows the financial impact. Each holds a slice. None holds the whole.

We believe the response to operational coordination is not to centralize what is inherently distributed. Build a shared record where local actors, humans and agents, read the same context, take locally correct actions, and write back transitions that other actors can see.

When the owner, next step, context, and proof are clear, agents and humans make locally correct decisions. When they are not, the work stops moving, even when every component still works.

We believe in operational memory through shared records, not another place to chase. Persistent context, reactive agents, structured transitions, clear outcomes.

What the work needs to hold

Every piece of work Solden runs is a record. Live, observable, reversible.

State
The work's current position in its lifecycle. A typed transition validated centrally, with the policy version that authorized it.
Ownership
Who acts next. Ownership is explicit, enforceable, auditable.
Dependencies
What this work is waiting on. Vendor information. Second approval. Counterparty redlines. Security review sign-off.
Exceptions
What is stuck and why, surfaced as structured state with the context needed to resolve it.
History
Every transition. Every override. Every reversal. Every retry. Reconstructable. Auditable. Reversible.

The record is yours

We believe work in progress belongs to the operator, not to the tool that processes a slice of it.

The current back office stack fragments operational memory across vendors. Each tool holds its slice and walls it off behind a UI. Want to know where work is across functions? Open ten tabs. Want to audit a cross-functional decision? Reconstruct it from email threads. Want to migrate? Lose six months of operational memory.

We believe operational memory should live on a record the operator controls. A typed, versioned record of every piece of work the organization runs. Independent of the surfaces that render it. Independent of the agents that advance it.

We believe the durable advantage of the next decade is ownership of work in progress. Surfaces will commoditize. Agents will commoditize. The layer that compounds is the one that holds the work record: the current position, the ownership graph, the audit trail, the institutional memory.

We believe Solden should be removable. Components should remain whole if you ever take it out. The bond should be strong but not destructive. Lock-in is an architectural choice, and we have chosen against it.

The thesis is general. The record, the bonding layer, all of it applies wherever back office work moves through fragmented systems. But a thesis must be proven where the cost of failure is highest before it can be claimed where the cost is broader. That is why we start in finance.

Finance is the wedge. The pattern generalizes.

We start in finance because finance cannot tolerate workflow ambiguity. A delayed invoice means a missed early-pay discount. A duplicate payment means audit exposure. A stalled close means a delayed earnings report.

But the architecture is not finance-specific. The state machine does not care about the domain. It cares about the parts: long-lived state, accountable transitions, structured exceptions, reversibility, audit trail.

The architecture that runs AP runs procurement. A purchase order moves from requested to quoted to approved to ordered to received to reconciled. Same shape. Same parts. Same broken coordination today.

The architecture that runs procurement runs compliance. An attestation moves from assigned to drafted to reviewed to approved to submitted to audited. Same shape.

The architecture that runs compliance runs vendor onboarding. A new vendor application moves from applied to diligence to security review to contracts to approved to live. Same shape.

The back office is one category, not many. Point tools serve one slice each. The record serves the work itself.

What we are building

A durable work record
Long-lived state, accountable transitions, structured exceptions, complete audit history.
A place for agents to work
Agents read structured state, propose transitions, advance work where policy allows. Humans intervene on exceptions.
A bonding layer across surfaces
Workflow state rendered inside the tools operators already use: inboxes, chat, ERPs, the systems your team already lives in.
Sovereign by design
The work record is the operator's. Not ours. Not the point tool's. Yours.

We believe in protocols, not platforms. In the long run, durable work records will look like Git: a layer every back office function builds on. We are starting with finance because finance is bleeding now. The pattern generalizes.

Those who saw it first

I

The unknown smiths of Mesopotamia

They alloyed tin and copper into bronze around 3000 BCE, the first deliberate bonding metal. Before them, civilizations were limited to what could be hammered or tied.

II

Friedrich Hayek

He proved that coordination through distributed local knowledge outperforms central planning. The price system, applied to operations, is the coordination pattern.

III

Henry Petroski

He showed that structures fail at the joints, not at the components. Operational systems fail the same way.

IV

Linus Torvalds

He built Git by treating change as the unit, not the artifact. Every operational transition should be audited and reversible.

V

Eric Brewer

He forced an honest reckoning with consistency, availability, and partition tolerance in distributed systems. Back office workflows have the same constraints.

VI

Donald Knuth

He treated correctness as a precondition, not a feature. Operational state needs the same standard.
